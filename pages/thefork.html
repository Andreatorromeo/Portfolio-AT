MobileApp
TheFork
A new restaurant experience
Wether you’re looking to book a romantic dinner, an exotic night out, or a relaxing dinner with friends, you will find every option available on TheFork. An easy to use design allows foodies to filter restaurants based on preference, consult reviews and instantly book online.
Case summary

Product vision

In 2007, when Steven Premutico founded Dimmi (TheFork), his main inspiration was the enhancement of the moment when we find our- selves around a table sharing food. Nowadays in a few seconds using our smartphones we can book a table, understand if the restaurant has our favorite foods and know how to get there. TheFork tries to simplify these steps and tries to give the best booking experience for the user, removing the most annoying part of a dinner or a lunch.

Challenges

User standards are always increasing, precisely because the needs are manifold and finding a common denominator between users becomes extremely complex. Finding and introducing features which cover the most required needs of specific users can become difficult, especially without transforming the global user experience in a painful journey.

Outcomes

Based on the research and the frictions between the app and the user, our team come up with some interesting features such as the preorder, a new batch for the authentic cuisine and new filters during the food research. Preordering means choosing and paying the meals for the registered guests and yourself when finalizing the booking process. Upon arriving to the restaurant, your meals will arrive to your table without any waiting time.

Research

After comprehending the generic usage of TheFork, we started with the first users testing to understand any kind of friction and the eventual presence of what are commonly called “Gulfs of Execution” and “Gulf of Evaluation”.

We read the reviews to have a clue on some direct feedback of typical users and we conducted new researches basing on the reviews results. On the other hand we conducted surveys and interviews to confirm the results obtained through the user testing and by reading the reviews.

One of the significant things we discovered, is that around 30% of bookings are made for the upcoming three hours and a large part of those users are workers and often, they have a small time window for eating and they don’t want to wait for the preparation of the meal upon arriving to the restaurant.

A large part of the users used the app abroad or in different cities and wanted to try typical cuisine. TheFork doesn’t give the possibility to find restaurants that offer typical dishes of the user’s specific location. The user has to look for pictures and the menu of the restaurant and the research could get boring, time-consuming and couldn’t yield any result

Empathizing
Basing on demographic and ethnographic results we were able to create three different personas. Travelers looking for typical cuisine, workers with tight schedules and people who change their plan. Our research and design were developed before the update of TheFork which introduced the possibility to edit a reservation.
From flows to prototyping

As always the flows were followed by lo-fi wireframes but in this case we had many limitations due to the fact we weren't redisigning the whole app. One of the most intricated part of the designing process was the prototyping. We had to link between them many screens and often they were nested.

Validation
Our final prototype was validated through user testing which involved 7 different users. We pointed out different tasks with different goals. The task needed a scenario to motivate user, avoiding abstract situation. We observed almost entirely positive feedback, especially for the “preorder” feature which was the most complex.
